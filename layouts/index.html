Rootless Root

This class aims to help students gain a greater understanding and appreciation of the tools that underlie modern computing. Although user-centric interfaces have become engineered, bloated, and unusable, the Unix shell will always be a source of comfort.

Once you grok Unix, you can begin to explore a plethora of other fields of development (which I hope to do as well) with relative ease, as you will have learned all of the skills to excel in them.

Intermediate to advanced programming experience (in any particular language) is almost certainly required. We will be going beyond language fundamentals and not stop to review them. If you can comfortably and quickly do ACSL or USACO programming questions, or you've worked on some significant side project, you're fine.

Expect this course to be extremely difficult and frustrating at times. It took me years of hard work to get to where I am right now, and in order to become skilled, you will need hours of practice and dedication. Random errors and not remembering basic tasks will be common, and you are expected to use your own problem-solving skills (and the power of the internet!) to figure out how to solve them. Raw intellect is less important than absolute persistence.

Remember that stack overflow, youtube, and technical documentation are your friends, and you should aim to use these resources more over time (and depend less on me). You are expected to buy a several-hundred-dollar Linux-compatible computer before joining this class, so you will commit to practicing on a regular basis. Messing around with your system is highly encouraged, the better if you break it!

Some specific topics will have short homework assignments with them, but many of them will be intentionally vague and open-ended; I want you to develop your own understanding of why the tools I teach you matter and how to creatively use them. In a culinary analogy, this is a class on how to cook, not how to make a specific pasta!

If you are excited about grokking the technology around you and you have the determination to do so while making fun and useful projects, you are in the right place. Good luck.

Help Policy (FAQ)

This is kind of a reiteration of points I make above, but I believe some emphasis should be made here.

I want to get you to the point where you don't have to be handheld. If you come to me with a question and it is abundantly clear that you have not tried to use external resources (documentation, google/duckduckgo/searx, stack overflow), I will refer you to appropriate resources with no further response. Questions on understanding documentation lingo or content, on the other hand, are welcome.

If you are afraid that whatever you are doing isn't the "right" way to do things, you should defer this judgement. Try to focus on getting things working, and if everything works, you found a valid solution. Focus on best practices after you know how to even get there; they'll come to you along the way.
